Team: Mohanty et al.

Note: Talk about greater performance and accuracy for newer models with citations and explanations. 
        Check if older models work for this citation

Finalised Citations List:
Gap Citations 
-	Mohanty et al.'s best-performing model achieved approximately 99% accuracy on curated PlantVillage images but only 31.4% and 31.7% accuracy on small real-world image datasets sourced from the internet, demonstrating a severe performance gap between benchmark and field conditions. 
-	PlantVillage images were collected under controlled laboratory conditions, creating the possibility that CNN models learn dataset-specific visual artifacts rather than genuine disease characteristics , a limitation the authors explicitly acknowledge. 
-	Controlled image acquisition conditions may substantially limit the generalizability of trained models to real agricultural environments. 
-	Even when the classification task was simplified to disease identification assuming known crop species, real-world performance remained substantially below benchmark accuracy , suggesting image conditions rather than task complexity are the primary driver of performance degradation.
-	Mohanty et al. state that a more diverse set of training data is needed to improve the accuracy on field-condition images, and that new image collection efforts should try to obtain images from many different perspectives, and ideally from settings that are as realistic as possible[Propose Synthetic Images as an alternative]

Informative Citations
-	Mohanty et al. evaluated CNN performance on color, grayscale, and leaf-segmented versions of the PlantVillage dataset specifically to investigate whether models were learning disease symptoms or dataset-specific visual artifacts such as uniform white backgrounds. 
-	Transfer learning from ImageNet consistently outperformed training from scratch across all architecture and dataset configurations tested. 
-	Color images achieved the highest mean F1 scores in benchmark testing, with grayscale images achieving a worst-case F1 of 0.8524, indicating that color information carries meaningful diagnostic signal for plant disease classification.
-	Mohanty et al. demonstrated that CNN-based plant disease classification achieves strong performance under ideal conditions, establishing the benchmark that subsequent robustness evaluations must be measured against. 



Background Citations
-	Adjusting the train-test split ratio in favour of testing reduced model accuracy, but not to a degree indicating significant overfitting, supporting the use of standard 80-20 training partitions. 
-	Mean precision, mean recall, and mean F1 score were used as standardised performance metrics, enabling consistent comparison across architectures and dataset configurations. 
-	Mohanty et al. note that while the training of the model takes a lot of time (multiple hours on a high performance GPU cluster computer), the classification itself is very fast (less than a second on a CPU), and can thus easily be implemented on a smartphone.

Methodology Citations
-	Mohanty et al. acquired real-world validation images through automated search retrieval combined with manual visual verification, obtaining two small datasets of 121 and 119 images respectively , demonstrating a replicable approach to assembling field-condition test sets without large-scale data collection.
